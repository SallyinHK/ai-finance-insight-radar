Step 1: Project Brief & User Definition

Product Name: AI Finance Insight Radar

Project Objective:
The objective is to design and prototype a workflow-based AI system that monitors, filters, classifies, and synthesizes the latest developments in Artificial Intelligence. The system translates AI-related information into finance-relevant business insights and generates professional LinkedIn thought leadership content. The final prototype is presented as a web-based deliverables container that documents the workflow design, prompt samples, generated content, and progress report.

Industry & Domain Definition:
The selected industry is Finance and Financial Services. The domain focus is AI business applications in investment research, risk management, compliance, operating model transformation, and fintech innovation.

Target User & Output Audience:
The primary user is a junior finance or strategy analyst who needs to monitor AI developments and convert them into structured insights for internal discussions, client-facing preparation, or professional content creation.

The target output audience includes financial analysts, asset managers, fintech founders, consultants, and finance executives who are interested in the business implications of AI.

Problem Statement:
The financial industry faces severe information overload around AI developments. Junior analysts often need to follow reports, product announcements, expert opinions, and market commentary from multiple sources, but not every AI update has clear relevance to finance. The challenge is not only finding information, but identifying which developments affect investment research, risk governance, operating efficiency, or financial product innovation.

AI Finance Insight Radar addresses this problem by creating a structured workflow that screens AI information through a finance-specific relevance router, converts high-value items into Insight Cards, classifies them by business impact, and generates LinkedIn-ready content using a researched KOL writing checklist.

Step 2: Workflow Architecture

AI Finance Insight Radar operates as a multi-stage workflow that transforms raw AI information into structured business insights and professional LinkedIn content. The architecture is designed to balance automation with human judgment, especially because financial content requires source credibility, careful interpretation, and tone control.

Module 1: Source Monitoring & Ingestion

Action:
The system monitors six categories of high-quality sources:

  1. Venture capital firms, such as a16z, Sequoia, and Y Combinator
  2. Investment banks and financial institutions, such as Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and Morgan Stanley
  3. AI thought leaders and business KOLs, such as Andrew Ng, Andrej Karpathy, Linas Beliūnas, Lex Sokolin, Ethan Mollick, and other finance- or AI-focused professionals
  4. Business schools and academic publications, such as Harvard Business Review, MIT Technology Review, MIT Sloan Management Review, and Stanford-related publications
  5. Management consulting firms, such as McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Deloitte, and Accenture
  6. Leading technology companies, such as OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, and NVIDIA

Output:
Raw articles, reports, blog posts, product announcements, interviews, and expert commentary.

Module 2: Knowledge Repository

Action:
All collected information is stored in a structured repository. Each item includes metadata such as source type, title, URL, publication date, summary, relevance score, category, generated insight card, content draft, and human review status.

Output:
A searchable knowledge base. A regular database is sufficient for the prototype, while a vector database can be added in the future to support semantic search across historical AI insights.

Revised LinkedIn Post Style & Anatomy Checklist

This checklist is derived from the KOL research above and will be used by the LinkedIn Content Generator.

1. Start with a specific tension

Avoid generic openings such as:

AI is transforming finance.

Use one of these instead:

A data-led hook

A workflow pain point

A practical question

A reversal of a common assumption

2. Make the business implication visible quickly

Within the first few lines, the reader should understand why this matters for finance, investment research, operating efficiency, risk, or fintech business models.

3. Use concrete workflow language

Generic AI claims should be translated into actual work processes:

deal screening

earnings transcript review

research backlog

compliance audit trail

approval chain

data source verification

4. Be opinionated, but not dramatic

The post can have a point of view, but it should avoid unsupported certainty. This is especially important for financial services, where content should not sound like investment advice or exaggerated prediction.

5. Include limitations or review points

A stronger post does not pretend AI has solved everything. It should mention at least one implementation issue, such as:

source reliability

hallucination risk

legacy system integration

accountability

auditability

data privacy

6. Use a visual that supports the argument

The image should not be generic AI stock art. It should function like a business communication asset.

Recommended visual formats:

workflow diagram

dashboard mockup

audit-trail diagram

data-led infographic

before-and-after operating model

7. End with a useful discussion point

Avoid forced engagement such as:

Thoughts?

Agree?

What do you think?

Use a more professional closing instead:

The real question is not whether the tool is impressive, but where it fits into the workflow.

or:

For finance teams, the next test is whether the output can be trusted, traced, and reviewed.

Final LinkedIn Text Draft

The easiest AI project to approve is often the least useful one.

A team finds a repetitive task.
They add an AI tool.
The task becomes faster.

That is useful, but it is not the same as changing the operating model.

In financial services, the larger productivity question is usually not:

“How many hours can this save?”

It is closer to:

“What should the workflow look like now that some work can be done differently?”

For example, if AI is added to a middle-office process, the team still needs to decide:

  • where human judgment is required
  • which outputs need review before they are trusted
  • what data the tool is allowed to access
  • how exceptions are escalated
  • who is accountable when the output is wrong

Without these decisions, AI can simply make an old process move faster.

That may create efficiency.
It may also create faster confusion.

The better use case is not only automation. It is redesigning the workflow around what AI can and cannot do well.

Step 6: Prompt Design Samples

Purpose of the Prompt Library

The prompt library defines how each module in AI Finance Insight Radar processes information. It turns the workflow from a conceptual framework into a repeatable system.

Each prompt is designed to improve three things:

  1. Business relevance
    The system should translate AI developments into finance-specific implications.
  2. Consistency
    The same type of input should produce comparable outputs, especially in relevance scoring, classification, and content generation.
  3. Reviewability
    The outputs should be structured so that a human analyst can check the source, logic, tone, and risk before publication.

The prompts also follow a strict anti-hallucination rule: the system should not invent source details, financial data, engagement metrics, author names, or publication dates.

Step 7: Progress Report

1. Workflow Architecture and Design Logic

AI Finance Insight Radar was designed as a workflow-based AI prototype for junior finance and strategy analysts. The purpose of the system is not simply to generate LinkedIn posts, but to create a structured process that transforms AI-related information into finance-relevant, reviewable, and publishable business insight.

The final workflow follows this structure:

Source Monitoring

↓

Knowledge Repository

↓

Summarization Agent

↓

Finance Relevance Router

↓

Insight Card Generator

↓

Business Classification Agent

↓

KOL Style Application

↓

LinkedIn Content Generator

↓

Human Review / System Audit

↓

Image Prompt Generator

↓

Final LinkedIn Content

The core design logic is that content quality depends on the quality of the upstream decision process. If the system moves directly from “AI news” to “LinkedIn post,” the final output is likely to become generic, promotional, or insufficiently grounded.

Therefore, the workflow adds several control points before final content generation:

  • The Source Monitoring Agent checks whether the source belongs to the monitoring scope.
  • The Knowledge Repository stores source metadata, summaries, relevance scores, categories, generated content, and review status.
  • The Finance Relevance Router decides whether the information is worth processing.
  • The Insight Card Generator translates the AI update into a finance-specific business implication.
  • The Business Classification Agent groups the insight into a clear business category.
  • The KOL Style Application Layer guides the writing and visual structure.
  • The Human Review / System Audit Layer checks tone, source reliability, risk, and overstatement before final output.

This design is especially important in financial services. A polished post is not enough. The content must also be traceable, measured, and suitable for professional review.

2. Key Challenges Encountered

Challenge 1: Avoiding a Generic AI News Summarizer

The first challenge was that the system could easily become a general AI news summarizer. This would not satisfy the assignment because the workflow needs to evaluate relevance to a professional role, industry, and business domain.

To address this, the project narrowed the target user to junior finance and strategy analysts. This made the routing and classification criteria more concrete. Instead of asking whether a piece of AI news is generally “interesting,” the system asks whether it affects:

investment research

risk and compliance

operating model transformation

fintech product development

cost structure

decision-making workflow

source verification

auditability

This helped the workflow become more business-oriented and less technology-driven.

Challenge 2: Making the Relevance Router Consistent

The initial relevance router used a 0–5 scoring system, but the meaning of each score was not clearly defined. Without calibration, the same type of information could receive different scores across different runs.

The final version added a scoring calibration:

5 = Direct and immediate impact on financial workflows, decision-making, risk, cost, or revenue.

3 = Indirect, emerging, or future impact on finance, but still relevant enough to monitor.

1 = General AI or technology news with no clear finance-specific implication.

0 = Not relevant to finance or outside the monitoring scope.

This change made the router more consistent and reduced the risk of overrating general AI news simply because the topic sounded important.

Challenge 3: Ensuring Data Provenance and Mitigating Hallucination

A major challenge in applying generative AI to finance-related content is the risk of fabricated data, unsupported claims, or unclear source attribution. Financial thought leadership cannot rely on vague or unverified statements, especially when discussing investment research, risk, compliance, or market implications.

In early prompt testing, the system showed a tendency to produce confident statements even when source details were incomplete. For example, it could overstate the relevance of a technology update, imply stronger business impact than the source supported, or produce generic references to “market data” without a clear origin.

To address this, several safeguards were added:

  • The Source Monitoring Prompt explicitly instructs the system not to invent missing dates, URLs, author names, or publication details.
  • The Summarization Prompt requires the system to separate confirmed facts from unclear or missing details.
  • The Insight Card retains source type and includes risk or limitation fields.
  • The System Flag highlights compliance, auditability, data privacy, or deployment warnings.
  • The Human Review / System Audit Layer checks whether any claim is overstated or unsupported before publication.

This optimization made the workflow more suitable for finance-related use cases. The system is not designed to produce content that sounds confident at all costs. It is designed to produce content that can be checked, traced, and responsibly revised.

Challenge 4: Preventing LinkedIn Posts from Sounding Too AI-Generated

The early LinkedIn drafts had a clear structure but sounded too polished and slightly generic. Some phrases, such as “fundamentally changing,” “sustainable competitive advantage,” and “the primary barrier,” made the content feel more like a consulting brochure than a realistic LinkedIn post.

To improve this, the writing style was adjusted in three ways:

  1. Claims were softened to avoid overstatement.
  2. Abstract business phrases were replaced with workflow-specific language.
  3. Posts were made less symmetrical, so they would not all follow the same formula.

For example, instead of saying:

AI will fundamentally transform due diligence.

the final version says:

AI may make the first draft of research faster. But conviction still needs to be earned by people.

The revised version is more measured and more appropriate for a finance audience.

Challenge 5: Making KOL Research Visible in the Workflow

The assignment requires LinkedIn content research based on 2–5 KOLs. A major risk was treating KOL analysis as a writing preference rather than a formal part of the workflow.

The project therefore made KOL research a visible system layer. The final version selected KOLs more closely aligned with the project direction:

Linas Beliūnas — fintech and AI business communication

Lex Sokolin — investment research, fintech, AI, and venture analysis

Ethan Mollick — AI productivity, experimentation, and workflow redesign

The system does not copy their wording. Instead, it extracts structural patterns:

  • Linas Beliūnas: sharp hook, compressed business implication, text-led visual.
  • Lex Sokolin: concrete investment workflow problem and deeper analysis.
  • Ethan Mollick: practical experimentation, workflow redesign, and limitation-setting.

These patterns are then used to guide the LinkedIn content generator.

Challenge 6: Connecting Text Generation with Image Generation

Another issue was that the first version of the checklist focused mostly on writing, while the assignment requires LinkedIn content with both text and image.

The final checklist added a visual strategy rule:

Avoid generic stock photos, robot hands, glowing brains, or futuristic clichés.

Use workflow diagrams, dashboard mockups, audit-trail diagrams, data-led infographics, or before-and-after operating model visuals.

The image prompt generator also requires:

Aspect ratio 4:5 or 1:1, high contrast, legible typography optimized for mobile feed.

This makes the visual output more consistent with professional LinkedIn content and more suitable for mobile viewing.

6. Lessons Learned

Lesson 1: Workflow Design Matters More Than the Final Post

A strong LinkedIn post is not only a writing problem. It depends on source selection, relevance scoring, classification, insight framing, and review. The workflow needs to make each upstream decision visible.

Lesson 2: Domain Specificity Improves Output Quality

When the system was defined too broadly, the output became generic. Once the target user was narrowed to junior finance and strategy analysts, the content became easier to evaluate and improve.

Lesson 3: Human Review Is Not a Weakness

The system is not designed to replace analyst judgment. The Human Review / System Audit layer improves credibility by checking whether the AI output is overstated, unsupported, or too generic.

Lesson 4: KOL Research Should Guide Structure, Not Copy Voice

The selected KOLs provide useful patterns, such as data-led hooks, workflow specificity, and practical experimentation. However, the final posts need to remain original and appropriate for a finance audience.

Lesson 5: Image Prompts Need Business Logic

A professional LinkedIn image should not just look good. It should support the argument of the post. For this project, workflow diagrams, dashboards, audit trails, and data-led infographics are more useful than generic AI imagery.

Lesson 6: Confidence Without Traceability Is a Risk

A generated post may sound polished even when its underlying support is weak. This is especially dangerous in finance-related content. The workflow therefore needs to preserve source type, uncertainty, limitations, and audit notes throughout the process.

7. Limitations of the Current System

The current prototype still has several limitations.

First, the system can support relevance scoring, but it cannot fully replace human judgment. This is especially clear in the Risk, Compliance & Governance dimension. For entirely new AI capabilities, the model may struggle to identify secondary compliance risks, such as auditability gaps, data permission issues, or downstream accountability problems.

Second, the quality of the workflow depends heavily on the quality of the source input. If the original source is incomplete, promotional, or lacks technical detail, the system can only flag the uncertainty; it cannot independently verify the full context without additional research.

Third, the KOL-based writing checklist improves structure and readability, but it does not guarantee audience engagement. LinkedIn performance depends on timing, network effects, personal credibility, topic relevance, and platform distribution, which are outside the control of the workflow.

Fourth, the current prototype demonstrates the workflow logic and output quality, but it is not yet a fully automated production system. Source monitoring, database storage, and publishing approval would need more robust implementation before real deployment.

These limitations reinforce the design principle that AI Finance Insight Radar should remain a copilot system rather than a fully autonomous publishing pipeline. The workflow can improve monitoring, classification, drafting, and review efficiency, but final interpretation and publication decisions should remain with a human analyst.

9. Final Reflection

The main improvement of AI Finance Insight Radar is that it treats content generation as a controlled business workflow rather than a one-step writing task.

The system does not assume that every AI update deserves attention. It first asks whether the update is relevant to finance, whether it has a clear business implication, whether the source is traceable, and whether the insight can be expressed responsibly. Only after passing these stages does the system generate LinkedIn content.

This approach is especially important in financial services. A polished post is not enough. The content also needs to be grounded, traceable, measured, and reviewed.

The final prototype therefore demonstrates both the creative side of generative AI and the governance logic needed to use it in a professional business context. It shows how AI can help analysts monitor information, classify insights, and draft thought leadership content, while still preserving human responsibility over interpretation, risk, and publication.
